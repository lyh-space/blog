2. First-step RSB

回顾使用复本对称假设之前的自由能 \[ \begin{aligned} \beta [f] =\lim_{n \rightarrow 0}\left\{-\frac{\beta^2 J^2}{4} +\frac{\beta^2 J^2}{2 n} \sum_{\alpha<\beta}q_{\alpha \beta}^2 +\frac{\beta J_0}{2 n} \sum m_\alpha^2 \right. -\left.\frac{1}{n} \log \mathrm{Tr~} \exp (L) \right\} \end{aligned} \] 其中， \[ \begin{equation} L=\beta^2 J^2 \sum_{\alpha<\beta} q_{\alpha \beta} S_i^\alpha S_i^\beta +\beta \sum\left(J_0 m_\alpha+h\right) S_i^\alpha \end{equation} \] 在1RSB中，仍保留 \(m_\alpha\) 的复本对称假设，有 \[ \lim _{n \rightarrow 0} \frac{1}{n} \sum_{\alpha \neq \beta} q_{\alpha \beta}^2=\lim _{n \rightarrow 0} \frac{1}{n}\left\{n^2 q_0^2+\frac{n}{m_1} m_1^2\left(q_1^2-q_0^2\right)-n q_1^2\right\}=\left(m_1-1\right) q_1^2-m_1 q_0^2 \] 以及 \[ \sum_{\alpha<\beta} q_{\alpha \beta} S^\alpha S^\beta=\frac{1}{2}\left\{q_0\left(\sum^n S^\alpha\right)^2+\left(q_1-q_0\right) \sum_{b=1}^{n / m_1}\left(\sum_{\alpha \in B_b}^{m_1} S^\alpha\right)^2-n q_1\right\} \] 可得 \[ \begin{aligned} \beta [f_{1RSB}] =\frac{\beta^2 J^2}{4} \Big[ \left(m_1-1\right) q_1^2-m_1 q_0^2 -1 \Big] + \frac{\beta^2 J^2}{2} m^2- \lim_{n \rightarrow 0}\frac{1}{n} \log \mathrm{Tr~} \exp (L_{1RSB}) \end{aligned} \] 其中 \[ L_{1RSB} = \frac{\beta^2 J^2}{2}\left\{q_0\left(\sum^n S^\alpha\right)^2+\left(q_1-q_0\right) \sum_{b=1}^{n / m_1}\left(\sum_{\alpha \in B_b}^{m_1} S^\alpha\right)^2-n q_1\right\} +\beta \sum\left(J_0 m+h\right) S_i^\alpha \] 利用H-S变换 \[ \exp \left[ \frac{\beta^2 J^2 q_0}{2} \left( \sum^n S^\alpha \right)^2 \right] = \int Du ~\exp\left( \beta J \sqrt{q_0} u \sum^n S^\alpha \right) \] 以及 \[ \begin{aligned} \exp \left[ \frac{\beta^2 J^2 \left( q_1-q_0 \right) }{2} \sum_{b=1}^{n / m_1}\left( \sum_{\alpha\in B_b}^{m_1} S^\alpha \right)^2 \right]&=\prod_{b=1}^{n / m_1} \exp \left[ \frac{\beta^2 J^2 \left( q_1-q_0 \right)}{2} \left( \sum_{\alpha\in B_b}^{m_1} S^\alpha \right)^2 \right] \\ &=\prod_{b=1}^{n / m_1} \int Dv_b ~\exp \left(\beta J \sqrt{q_1-q_0} ~v_b\sum_{\alpha\in B_b}^{m_1} S^\alpha \right) \end{aligned} \]

3. Stability of the first step RSB

3.2.3 Stability of the first step RSB The stability of 1RSB can be investigated by a direct generalization of the argument in \(\S 3.1\). We mention only a few main points for the case \(J_0=h=0\). It is sufficient to treat two cases: one with all indices \(\alpha, \beta, \gamma, \delta\) of the Hessian elements within the same block and the other with indices in two different blocks.

If \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) in \(q_{\alpha \beta}\) belong to the same block, the stability condition of the replicon mode for infinitesimal deviations from 1RSB is expressed as \[ \lambda_3=P-2 Q+R=1-\beta^2 J^2 \int \mathrm{D} u \frac{\int \mathrm{D} v \cosh ^{m_1-4} \Xi}{\int \mathrm{D} v \cosh ^{m_1} \Xi}>0 . \] For the replicon mode between two different blocks, the stability condition reads \[ \lambda_3=P-2 Q+R=1-\beta^2 J^2 \int \mathrm{D} u\left(\frac{\int \mathrm{D} v \cosh ^{m_1-1} \Xi}{\int \mathrm{D} v \cosh ^{m_1} \Xi}\right)^4>0 . \] According to the Schwarz inequality, the right hand side of (3.35) is less than or equal to that of (3.36), and therefore the former is sufficient. Equation (3.35) is not satisfied in the spin glass phase similar to the case of the RSB solution. However, the absolute value of the eigenvalue is confirmed by numerical evaluation to be smaller than that of the RS solution although \(\lambda_3\) is still negative. This suggests an improvement towards a stable solution. The entropy per spin at \(J_0=0, T=0\) reduces from \(-0.16(=-1 / 2 \pi)\) for the RS solution to -0.01 for the 1RSB. Thus we may expect to obtain still better results if we go further into replica symmetry breaking.

4. Full RSB solution

3.3 Full RSB solution Let us proceed with the calculation of the free energy (2.17) by a multiple-step RSB. We restrict ourselves to the case \(J_0=0\) for simplicity. 3.3.1 Physical quantities The sum involving \(q_{\alpha \beta}^2\) in the free energy (2.17) can be expressed at the \(K\) th step of RSB ( \(K\)-RSB) as follows by counting the number of elements in a similar way to the 1RSB case (3.29): \[ \begin{aligned} & \sum_{\alpha \neq \beta} q_{\alpha \beta}^l \\ & =q_0^l n^2+\left(q_1^l-q_0^l\right) m_1^2 \cdot \frac{n}{m_1}+\left(q_2^l-q_1^l\right) m_2^2 \cdot \frac{m_1}{m_2} \cdot \frac{n}{m_1}+\cdots-q_K^l \cdot n \\ & =n \sum_{j=0}^K\left(m_j-m_{j+1}\right) q_j^l, \end{aligned} \] where \(l\) is an arbitrary integer and \(m_0=n, m_{K+1}=1\). In the limit \(n \rightarrow 0\), we may use the replacement \(m_j-m_{j+1} \rightarrow-\mathrm{d} x\) to find

\[ \frac{1}{n} \sum_{\alpha \neq \beta} q_{\alpha \beta}^l \rightarrow-\int_0^1 q^l(x) \mathrm{d} x \] The internal energy for \(J_0=0, h=0\) is given by differentiation of the free energy (2.17) by \(\beta\) as \(^2\) \[ E=-\frac{\beta J^2}{2}\left(1+\frac{2}{n} \sum_{\alpha<\beta} q_{\alpha \beta}^2\right) \rightarrow-\frac{\beta J^2}{2}\left(1-\int_0^1 q^2(x) \mathrm{d} x\right) . \] The magnetic susceptibility can be written down from the second derivative of (2.17) by \(h\) as \[ \chi=\beta\left(1+\frac{1}{n} \sum_{\alpha \neq \beta} q_{\alpha \beta}\right) \rightarrow \beta\left(1-\int_0^1 q(x) \mathrm{d} x\right) . \] It needs some calculations to derive the free energy in the full RSB scheme. Details are given in Appendix B. The final expression of the free energy (2.17) is \[ \beta f=-\frac{\beta^2 J^2}{4}\left\{1+\int_0^1 q(x)^2 \mathrm{~d} x-2 q(1)\right\}-\int \mathrm{D} u f_0(0, \sqrt{q(0)} u) . \] Here \(f_0\) satisfies the Parisi equation \[ \frac{\partial f_0(x, h)}{\partial x}=-\frac{J^2}{2} \frac{\mathrm{d} q}{\mathrm{~d} x}\left\{\frac{\partial^2 f_0}{\partial h^2}+x\left(\frac{\partial f_0}{\partial h}\right)^2\right\} \] to be solved under the initial condition \(f_0(1, h)=\log 2 \cosh \beta h\).

3.3.2 Order parameter near the critical point It is in general very difficult to find a solution to the extremization condition of the free energy (3.41) with respect to the order function \(q(x)\). It is nevertheless possible to derive some explicit results by the Landau expansion when the temperature is close to the critical point and consequently \(q(x)\) is small. Let us briefly explain the essence of this procedure.

When \(J_0=h=0\), the expansion of the free energy (2.17) to fourth order in \(q_{\alpha \beta}\) turns out to be \[ \begin{aligned} \beta f= & \lim _{n \rightarrow 0} \frac{1}{n}\left\{\frac{1}{4}\left(\frac{T^2}{T_{\mathrm{f}}^2}-1\right) \operatorname{Tr} Q^2-\frac{1}{6} \operatorname{Tr} Q^3\right. \\ & \left.-\frac{1}{8} \operatorname{Tr} Q^4+\frac{1}{4} \sum_{\alpha \neq \beta \neq \gamma} Q_{\alpha \beta}^2 Q_{\alpha \gamma}^2-\frac{1}{12} \sum_{\alpha \neq \beta} Q_{\alpha \beta}^4\right\}, \end{aligned} \] where we have dropped \(q\)-independent terms. The operator Tr here denotes the diagonal sum in the replica space. We have introduced the notation \(Q_{\alpha \beta}=(\beta J)^2 q_{\alpha \beta}\). Only the last term is relevant to the RSB. It can indeed be verified that the eigenvalue of the replicon mode that determines stability of the RS solution is, by setting the coefficient of the last term to \(-y\) (which is actually \(-1 / 12)\) \[ \lambda_3=-16 y \theta^2, \] where \(\theta=\left(T_{\mathrm{f}}-T\right) / T_{\mathrm{f}}\). We may thus neglect all fourth-order terms except \(Q_{\alpha \beta}^4\) to discuss the essential features of the RSB and let \(n \rightarrow 0\) to get \[ \beta f=\frac{1}{2} \int_0^1 \mathrm{~d} x\left\{|\theta| q^2(x)-\frac{1}{3} x q^3(x)-q(x) \int_0^x q^2(y) \mathrm{d} y+\frac{1}{6} q^4(x)\right\} . \] The extremization condition with respect to \(q(x)\) is written explicitly as \[ 2|\theta| q(x)-x q^2(x)-\int_0^x q^2(y) \mathrm{d} y-2 q(x) \int_x^1 q(y) \mathrm{d} y+\frac{2}{3} q^3(x)=0 . \] Differentiation of this formula gives \[ |\theta|-x q(x)-\int_x^1 q(y) \mathrm{d} y+q^2(x)=0 \quad \text { or } \quad q^{\prime}(x)=0 . \] Still further differentiation leads to \[ q(x)=\frac{x}{2} \quad \text { or } \quad q^{\prime}(x)=0 . \] The RS solution corresponds to a constant \(q(x)\). This constant is equal to \(|\theta|\) according to (3.46). There also exists an \(x\)-dependent solution \[ \begin{aligned} & q(x)=\frac{x}{2} \quad\left(0 \leq x \leq x_1=2 q(1)\right) \\ & q(x)=q(1) \quad\left(x_1 \leq x \leq 1\right) . \end{aligned} \] By inserting this solution in the variational condition (3.46), we obtain \[ q(1)=|\theta|+\mathcal{O}\left(\theta^2\right) \] Figure 3.2 shows the resulting behaviour of \(q(x)\) near the critical point where \(\theta\) is close to zero.

The susceptibility is a constant \(\chi=1 / J\) near the critical point \(T_{\mathrm{f}}\) because the integral in (3.40) is \(1-T / T_{\mathrm{f}}\) according to (3.49)-(3.51). It turns out in fact that this result remains valid not only near \(T_{\mathrm{f}}\) but over the whole temperature range below the critical point. We use this fact to show that the phase boundary between the spin glass and ferromagnetic phases is a vertical line at \(J_0=J\) as in Fig. 2.1 (Toulouse 1980).

The Hamiltonian of the SK model (2.7) suggests that a change of the centre of distribution of \(J_{i j}\) from 0 to \(J_0 / N\) shifts the energy per spin by \(-J_0 \mathrm{~m}^2 / 2\). Thus the free energy \(f\left(T, m, J_0\right)\) as a function of \(T\) and \(m\) satisfies \[ f\left(T, m, J_0\right)=f(T, m, 0)-\frac{1}{2} J_0 m^2 . \] From the thermodynamic relation \[ \frac{\partial f(T, m, 0)}{\partial m}=h \] and the fact that \(m=0\) when \(J_0=0\) and \(h=0\), we obtain \[ \left.\left.\chi^{-1}=\left(\frac{\partial m}{\partial h}\right]_{h \rightarrow 0}\right)^{-1}=\frac{\partial^2 f(T, m, 0)}{\partial m^2}\right]_{m \rightarrow 0} . \] Thus, for sufficiently small \(m\), we have \[ f(T, m, 0)=f_0(T)+\frac{1}{2} \chi^{-1} m^2 . \] Combining (3.52) and (3.55) gives \[ f\left(T, m, J_0\right)=f_0(T)+\frac{1}{2}\left(\chi^{-1}-J_0\right) m^2 . \] This formula shows that the coefficient of \(m^2\) in \(f\left(T, m, J_0\right)\) vanishes when \(\chi=\) \(1 / J_0\) and therefore there is a phase transition between the ferromagnetic and non-ferromagnetic phases according to the Landau theory. Since \(\chi=1 / J\) in the whole range \(T<T_{\mathrm{f}}\), we conclude that the boundary between the ferromagnetic and spin glass phases exists at \(J_0=J\).

Stability analysis of the Parisi solution has revealed that the eigenvalue of the replicon mode is zero, implying marginal stability of the Parisi RSB solution. No other solutions have been found with a non-negative replicon eigenvalue of the Hessian.
